What We've Learned

Core principles and methodological practices that guide our approach to development research, refined through years of fieldwork across South Asian contexts.

Principles for Context-Sensitive Research

These learnings emerge from both successes and failures in our work. They represent our evolving understanding of how to conduct research that is both methodologically rigorous and ethically grounded in community needs.

Our Research Framework

Four foundational pillars that inform every research project we undertake.

Listen First

Begin every project by understanding community priorities, local knowledge systems, and existing solutions before imposing external frameworks.

Adapt Methods

Modify research tools and approaches based on cultural context, power dynamics, and participant needs rather than rigid adherence to standard protocols.

Share Power

Include communities as co-researchers and decision-makers throughout the research process, not just as data sources.

Act on Findings

Ensure research leads to tangible benefits for participants and communities, whether through policy change, program improvement, or capacity building.

1

Listen Before Measuring

Story from Chhattisgarh

In a climate adaptation study, official rainfall data suggested one intervention model. But conversations with farmers revealed micro-climate variations and traditional forecasting methods that completely changed our understanding of local water management needs. The community knowledge led to a more effective and locally-appropriate program design.

What This Means in Practice

  • Begin every project with open-ended conversations before developing research instruments
  • Create space for community members to identify priorities and define problems in their own terms
  • Map existing knowledge systems and solutions before proposing new interventions
  • Design research questions collaboratively rather than extractively
  • Validate external data sources against local knowledge and experience

Application Across Sectors

This approach has proven essential in health research (understanding care-seeking behavior), education studies (mapping informal learning systems), and livelihoods work (documenting traditional economic practices). Listening first consistently leads to more accurate data and more effective interventions.

2

Methods Must Serve Context, Not Vice Versa

Story from Lucknow

We started a program evaluation using a standard logframe approach that emphasized predetermined indicators. Community partners found this extractive and disconnected from their goals. Switching to collaborative question-mapping sessions produced a framework that was both more honest about uncertainties and more useful for adaptive programming.

Methodological Flexibility Principles

  • Adapt survey instruments to local languages, concepts, and communication styles
  • Modify consent processes to align with cultural norms while maintaining ethical standards
  • Integrate visual, audio, and participatory methods for populations with diverse literacy levels
  • Allow research timelines to accommodate community rhythms and seasonal patterns
  • Design feedback mechanisms that work within existing social structures

Balancing Rigor with Responsiveness

Methodological adaptation doesn't mean sacrificing rigor. Instead, it requires deeper engagement with what constitutes valid and reliable evidence within specific cultural contexts. This often produces more robust findings than rigid adherence to standardized protocols.

3

Change Is Never Linear

Story from Rural Madhya Pradesh

A school improvement program showed steady gains in enrollment for two years, then sudden drops. Linear impact models would have missed that state textbook policy changes were interacting with local economic pressures. Understanding non-linear change patterns led to program adaptations that addressed root causes rather than symptoms.

Embracing Complexity in Research Design

  • Design evaluation frameworks that can capture unexpected outcomes and feedback loops
  • Include multiple measurement points to understand change trajectories, not just end points
  • Map external factors and contextual changes that might influence program outcomes
  • Create space for participant-defined indicators of change that may not follow linear patterns
  • Document both intended and unintended consequences of interventions

Implications for Program Learning

Non-linear change patterns require research designs that support adaptive programming. This means building in regular reflection cycles, maintaining flexible data collection systems, and creating feedback loops that allow programs to adjust strategies based on emerging evidence.

4

Consent and Context Must Evolve Together

Story from Maharashtra

In a disability inclusion survey, standard consent forms created barriers because they used technical language and assumed individual decision-making. Working with disabled people's organizations, we developed ongoing consent processes that respected both individual autonomy and family/community consultation practices. Participation increased and data quality improved significantly.

Culturally-Responsive Ethical Practices

  • Develop consent processes that align with local decision-making norms while protecting individual rights
  • Create multilingual and visual consent materials for diverse literacy levels
  • Establish ongoing consent protocols for longitudinal research relationships
  • Build community review processes into research design and implementation
  • Ensure data ownership and sharing agreements reflect community values and needs

Beyond Procedural Ethics

Ethical research requires more than institutional review board approval. It demands ongoing relationship-building, transparent communication about research benefits and risks, and genuine commitment to ensuring research serves participant communities.

5

Mixed Methods Must Be Truly Integrated

Story from Assam Tea Gardens

A maternal health evaluation showed positive quantitative outcomes, but ethnographic research revealed that the intervention was working well for permanent workers while failing temporary laborers. The quantitative data masked crucial equity dimensions that only became visible through integrated qualitative analysis. This led to program redesign that addressed structural inequalities.

Integration Strategies

  • Design qualitative and quantitative components to inform and validate each other
  • Create analysis frameworks that can synthesize different types of evidence
  • Use qualitative findings to interpret quantitative patterns and vice versa
  • Include community members in data interpretation and meaning-making processes
  • Report findings in ways that preserve the complexity revealed by mixed methods

Beyond Parallel Data Collection

True mixed methods integration requires iterative design where findings from one method shape data collection and analysis in another. This approach reveals how interventions work differently across populations and contexts, leading to more effective and equitable programming.

Our Signature Approach: Community-Led Research

We've developed a participatory research methodology that trains community members as co-researchers while maintaining academic rigor. This approach has proven particularly effective in conflict-affected areas, with marginalized populations, and for sensitive research topics.

Capacity Building

Community members develop research skills that serve long-term advocacy and evaluation needs.

Cultural Sensitivity

Local researchers understand context and can access information that would be difficult for outsiders to obtain.

Ethical Safeguards

Community-led research creates natural protections against extractive or harmful research practices.

Sustainable Impact

Research relationships continue beyond project timelines, supporting ongoing learning and adaptation.
